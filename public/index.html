GIS MODEL FOR SITING STORMWATER GREEN INFRASTRUCTURE PRACTICES

The Chesapeake Bay is at risk to coastal hazards such as hurricanes, severe storms, floods and sea level rise. Coastal communities such as Oxford, Maryland must understand these risks and plan for the future to protect its residents and infrastructure.

This GIS tool is a model that was developed to provide the Town of Oxford an interactive resource that
(1) prioritizes properties for targeting stormwater green infrastructure practices based on the likelihood of flooding from significant rain events and future flooding events from sea level rise and
(2) identifies the appropriate types of stormwater green infrastructure practices that can be located on a particular parcel to help manage flooding events based on soil types, ground water levels, size of parcel area and contributing runoff.

First, the user is welcomed by a simple topographic map of Oxford, MD.
The user can find parcels by owner's name using the Oxford Property Parcels drop-down in the tool's search bar.

After the parcel layer is displayed, the user can add the following Green Infrastructure Layers to see if their property parcel(s) qualify for a specific Best Management Practice 
(Example: Rain Gardens, Bioretention Area, Grass Channel).

Finally, the user can add Mean Sea Level Rise (MSL)
and/or Mean High Higher Water (MHHW) polygons to the map to see how the user's property parcel may be affected by the MSL and MHHW phenomena.  

Rain Gardens Parcels are areas where rain gardens are an appropriate stormwater management solution somewhere within that site based on factors such as
soil type and water depth.

Rain gardens are landscaped depressions that capture and treat stormwater runoff. They contain soil and herbaceous plants, allowing pollutant removal by the slow infiltration of runoff. A single site will often use several rain gardens.

Bioretention Area Parcels display parcels that contain suitable locations to treat stormwater using bioretention practices, based on factors such as drainage area,
soil type and water depth.

Bioretentions are landscaped depressions that include a filter bed of sand, soil and organic material that ponds stormwater and filters it through the bed. An underdrain is often another component of bioretentions.

Grass Channel Parcels are areas when grass channels may help address stormwater concerns somewhere within that site based on environmental factors.

Grass channels can provide a modest amount of stormwater capture and filtering, and are a good alternative to curbs and gutters.

Highlighted areas are locations throughout town where stormwater may cause flooding based on elevation, development, stormwater flow and the existence of natural features such as wetlands.

This layer ranks the priority of areas in town based on their vulnerability to existing and future coastal hazards based on projected sea level rise
and areas of minor coastal flooding.

Town planners may use this tool to help them prioritize actions based on a color coded scale of red (greater risk to coastal hazard), to orange, then yellow, then green.
Users can click on a single parcel to view who owns the property, its overall risk to hazards, its land use classification, and its exposure to both mean high high water and sea level rise.

The pop-up window also displays whether or not the parcel is an appropriate location for a grass channel, bioretention or rain garden.
